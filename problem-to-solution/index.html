    Problem-to-Solution Narratives — Agile, SWOT & GAP Analysis

    Rescuing a Stalled Digital Transformation Before It Became a Case Study in What Not to Do

    When a Strategy Exists on Paper But Not in Practice, the Gap Between Them Has a Cost

    The Challenge

    A mid-sized management consulting firm had made a significant commitment to modernizing its internal delivery model — replacing fragmented project tracking, siloed client reporting, and manual billing reconciliation with an integrated platform. The initiative had executive sponsorship, a vendor selected, and a budget approved. Twelve months in, almost nothing had been delivered.

    Teams were still working in the old tools. The vendor relationship was strained. Morale on the internal transformation team was low. Leadership was starting to ask whether the entire initiative should be abandoned — or whether the problem was fixable.

    The answer was that the problem was fixable, but only if someone was willing to honestly diagnose what had actually gone wrong.

    My Role

    I was brought in as a Business Analyst and facilitation lead during a project recovery engagement. My remit was to assess the current state, identify root causes of the stall, and co-design a revised delivery roadmap that the organization could realistically execute.

    What I Did

    Phase 1 — Honest Assessment

    Before any solution work began, the team needed an unvarnished picture of where things stood. I facilitated a structured SWOT Analysis with the transformation steering committee and core delivery team — separately first, then together. Running them separately was deliberate: it gave each group room to be candid before bringing perspectives into the same room.

    Phase 2 — GAP Analysis

    With the SWOT grounding the conversation, I led a structured GAP Analysis across five delivery dimensions: requirements clarity, stakeholder alignment, team structure, scope control, and change management. For each dimension, the team assessed current state, defined a realistic target state, and identified the actions needed to close the gap.

    Phase 3 — Agile Recovery Roadmap

    With root causes identified, I facilitated the design of a Kanban-based recovery roadmap that gave the organization visible forward momentum within the first 30 days — critical for restoring confidence — while protecting space for the foundational requirements work that had been skipped the first time around.

    • Established a Kanban board in Jira with four lanes: Backlog, In Requirements, In Development, and Done
    • Defined WIP limits for each lane to prevent the scope creep that had derailed the original effort
    • Worked with the vendor to re-baseline the delivery schedule around three focused phases rather than one large launch
    • Helped stakeholders celebrate early wins publicly — the billing module went live on time in Phase 1, which changed the narrative around the entire initiative

    The Outcome

    • The initiative moved from effectively stalled to Phase 1 delivered within 11 weeks of the recovery engagement beginning
    • Vendor relationship was preserved; the contract was renewed with a renegotiated scope and timeline
    • The Kanban framework was adopted as the firm's standard delivery tracking approach for all major internal initiatives
    • Internal adoption rate for the billing module hit 91% in the first quarter — the highest voluntary adoption rate of any internal tool rollout in the firm's recent history

    Learning Reflection

    The most important lesson here had nothing to do with a framework or a tool. The project stalled primarily because nobody had been assigned clear accountability for requirements work at the start. Frameworks don't fail — governance vacuums do. In any future engagement involving organizational change of this scale, I would make the assignment of a dedicated BA a formal precondition before any delivery work begins. That single structural decision would have prevented most of what went wrong.
