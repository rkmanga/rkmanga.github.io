    Writing the Blueprint for a Clinical Data System Migration

    When Patient Safety Depends on Getting the Requirements Right the First Time

    The Challenge

    A regional healthcare network was replacing its legacy clinical data management system — a platform more than a decade old, built up with layers of workarounds, custom fields, and undocumented processes nobody fully understood anymore. The replacement needed to preserve data integrity, satisfy HIPAA compliance requirements, and support the day-to-day workflows of nurses, lab technicians, physicians, and administrators — a stakeholder group with competing priorities and almost no time to spare.

    The risk of getting requirements wrong wasn't a business inconvenience. It was a patient safety concern.

    My Role

    I served as Lead Business Analyst for the requirements phase of the project. My responsibilities spanned stakeholder elicitation, authoring all requirements artifacts, and bridging the clinical teams' needs with the development team's sprint backlog.

    What I Did

    Elicitation - Conducted structured interviews with clinicians, lab technicians, compliance officers, and IT administrators - Ran two facilitated workshops to surface and align requirements across departments with competing priorities - Shifted from open-ended sessions to validation-based sessions — pre-drafting requirements for stakeholders to confirm, challenge, or correct — which dramatically improved both speed and quality

    Documentation - Authored the Business Requirements Document (BRD): covering data migration scope, user roles, access controls, reporting requirements, and EHR integration - Produced the Functional Requirements Document (FRD): translating each BRD item into specific, testable system behaviors - Wrote Agile User Stories for development sprint planning - Built wireframes for key UI screens in Miro to validate workflow assumptions before development began - Maintained a Requirements Traceability Matrix (RTM) to ensure every requirement was designed, built, and tested

    Sample User Stories

    Story ID User Story Acceptance Criteria
    US-014 As a lab technician, I want to enter specimen results directly into the patient record so that clinicians see real-time data without manual transcription. Results appear in patient record within 60 seconds; no duplicate entry required
    US-031 As a compliance officer, I want an automated audit log of all record access events so that I can respond to regulatory inquiries within required timeframes. Log captures user ID, timestamp, record accessed, and action taken; exportable to PDF and CSV
    US-047 As a physician, I want to view a patient's full lab history in a single timeline view so that I can identify trends without navigating multiple screens. Timeline displays all results chronologically; filterable by test type and date range

    The Outcome

    • System launched on schedule with zero major compliance findings at the post-go-live audit
    • Clinical staff adoption exceeded 85% in the first month — a result the project team attributed to user-centric design driven by thorough requirements work
    • Data entry error rate declined an estimated 22% compared to the legacy system
    • The RTM was cited by the compliance team as one of the most complete they had reviewed on a migration project of this scope

    Learning Reflection

    Clinicians are time-constrained in ways most stakeholders simply are not. My early elicitation sessions were too open-ended, which led to long, unfocused meetings and requirements that needed extensive rework. Switching to validation-based sessions was the turning point. I'd build that approach into my methodology from day one on any healthcare engagement going forward — it respects the stakeholder's time and produces better requirements.
