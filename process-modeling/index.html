    Mapping the Invisible: Redesigning a Claims Process Nobody Had Ever Drawn on Paper

    You Can't Fix What You Can't See — And Nobody Had Ever Drawn This Process on Paper

    The Challenge

    A property and casualty insurance carrier was experiencing a troubling pattern: claims that should have resolved in under two weeks were routinely stretching to six, eight, or even ten weeks. Customers were frustrated. Adjusters were buried. Compliance was flagging audit findings related to communication delays. And the operations team kept explaining the problem the same way — "it's just how the process works" — which is often the clearest possible signal that a process has never been formally examined.

    Leadership commissioned a process improvement initiative with one central goal: find out where claims were getting stuck and design a better path forward.

    My Role

    I served as the Business Analyst and process modeling lead. My responsibilities included leading the discovery effort, building the current-state and future-state process models, and working with the operations and IT teams to validate that the proposed redesign was both operationally realistic and technically feasible to support.

    What I Did

    Phase 1 — Process Discovery

    No formal process documentation existed. The process lived in the habits, workarounds, and institutional knowledge of the adjusters and operations staff who worked it every day. My first task was to make it visible.

    • Conducted ride-along observation sessions with claims adjusters, reviewing 15 live claims across three adjusters
    • Facilitated three process walkthroughs with cross-functional groups: adjusters, supervisors, legal, and IT
    • Used sticky-note mapping in Miro to capture the "as-is" flow before committing anything to a formal model
    • Identified 11 distinct handoff points in the current process — each one a potential delay

    Phase 2 — Bottleneck Analysis

    With the current state mapped, I used process mining techniques and historical claims data to quantify where time was actually being lost.

    HANDOFF POINT                   AVG. DELAY     ROOT CAUSE IDENTIFIED
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Intake → Adjuster Assignment     2.3 days       Manual queue, no routing rules
Info Request → Claimant          1.1 days       No standardized communication
                                                template; adjusters drafting manually
Adjuster → Supervisor (>$25K)    4.7 days       Supervisor review queue unmanaged;
                                                no SLA defined
Supervisor → Legal               6.2 days       No formal escalation trigger;
                                                adjuster judgment-based
Legal → Resolution               Variable       No tracking; cases fell into
                                                unstructured email workflow
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Total Avg. Unnecessary Delay:   ~14 days of the ~42-day average cycle time

    Phase 3 — Future-State Design

    With bottlenecks quantified and root causes understood, I facilitated the design of the future-state process. The redesign wasn't about adding technology — it was about adding structure, accountability, and clarity to a process that had grown organically without either.

    Key redesign decisions included: - Automated routing rules for adjuster assignment based on claim type and geography, eliminating the manual queue - Standardized communication templates for information requests with automated follow-up triggers - Defined SLAs at every handoff point with visibility in the claims management system - A structured escalation trigger for legal referrals — rule-based rather than adjuster-dependent — reducing subjective variation

    Future-State BPMN (Summary)

    [CLAIMANT] → [INTAKE: Auto-logged + routed in <4hrs] → [ADJUSTER: Assigned by rule, not manual queue]
    ↓
[Info Request? → Standardized template sent; auto-follow-up at Day 3 and Day 7]
    ↓
[Estimate → Supervisor queue with defined 48hr SLA for >$25K claims]
    ↓
[Approve → Auto-trigger payment | Dispute → Rule-based legal referral with full case file auto-packaged]
    ↓
[Legal → Tracked in system with milestone dates; no unstructured email workflow]

    The Outcome

    • Average claims cycle time dropped from 42 days to 23 days in the six months following the redesign implementation — a 45% reduction
    • The auto-routing rule eliminated the manual assignment queue, recovering approximately 2.3 days per claim immediately
    • Compliance audit findings related to communication delays were fully resolved in the next audit cycle
    • Adjuster satisfaction improved measurably — structured escalation rules removed a significant source of ambiguity and stress from their daily work
    • The future-state BPMN models were formally adopted as the carrier's documented standard operating procedure for the claims function — the first time the process had ever been officially documented

    Learning Reflection

    The single most impactful part of this engagement wasn't the redesign — it was the act of making the process visible in the first place. Once the current-state map existed, the bottlenecks were undeniable. Teams that had been debating solutions for months aligned quickly once they were all looking at the same picture. In future process improvement engagements, I'd invest even more time in the current-state modeling phase and make sure every key stakeholder validates the map before any solution conversation begins. Shared visibility is itself a form of alignment.
