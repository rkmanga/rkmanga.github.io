    From Scattered Data to a Single Source of Truth

    Rebuilding a SaaS Company's Knowledge Infrastructure Before It Became a Growth Liability

    The Challenge

    A fast-growing SaaS company had a knowledge problem hiding in plain sight. Product documentation lived in email threads, shared drives, outdated wikis, and the institutional memory of engineers who had been with the company since its earliest days. As the team scaled past 150 employees and the customer base expanded, support ticket volume climbed — not because the product was failing, but because nobody could find reliable answers fast enough.

    The company was spending money it didn't need to spend. Customers were frustrated in entirely preventable ways. Leadership knew something had to change, but didn't know where to start.

    My Role

    I was engaged as a Business Analyst embedded with the Product and Customer Success teams. My mandate was to assess the current state of the knowledge infrastructure, identify critical gaps, and design a scalable solution with requirements the internal team could build and maintain long after the engagement ended.

    What I Did

    The work unfolded in three phases.

    Phase 1 — Discover - Conducted 14 stakeholder interviews across Support, Engineering, Product, and Customer Success - Audited 300+ existing documents to assess accuracy, findability, and coverage - Mapped current documentation workflows to surface how content was actually being created, stored, and accessed

    Phase 2 — Diagnose - Performed a GAP analysis comparing current documentation capabilities against the needs of a scaling SaaS organization - Identified five critical gaps: no taxonomy, no content ownership model, no review cadence, no single access point, and no format or quality standards - Prioritized gaps using a MoSCoW framework with cross-functional input

    Phase 3 — Design - Authored a Business Requirements Document (BRD) covering the new knowledge base architecture and governance model - Designed a content taxonomy and metadata schema for searchability and discoverability - Collaborated with IT to configure a SharePoint-based internal knowledge hub - Developed a Documentation Governance Model defining ownership, review schedules, and quality standards

    The Outcome

    • Support ticket volume dropped approximately 30% within 90 days of the new knowledge hub going live
    • New employee onboarding was reduced from 3 weeks to under 10 days
    • The Documentation Governance Model was adopted company-wide as standard operating procedure
    • The support team's NPS contribution improved measurably in the quarter following launch

    Learning Reflection

    The governance model was sound — but it was designed after content migration had already started. In future projects, I'd establish ownership assignments and review cadences before any content moves. Documentation without assigned stewardship drifts toward obsolescence almost immediately, and a lifecycle plan is as important as the content itself.
